Unfortunately, under Denver’s Ordinance Sec. 8-55, “pit-bull type” breeds are banned from the City and animal protection can impound your dog and force you to relocate it or euthanize it. Rater Sheet

In a study published in 2008 by the Coalition for living Safely with dogs, dog bites were most common in the following breeds:

  • Labrador retrievers: 13.3%
  • Pit Bulls: 8.4%
  • German Shepherds: 7.8%
  • Rottweilers: 3.9%
  • Chows: 3.5%

Labradors lead the pack with the greatest number of attacks, which is a surprising discovery when considering the mild nature of the dogs. However, the number of dog bites is related to popularity of the breed: the more popular a dog is, the more likely one will have a negative encounter with a human. All breeds can be poorly trained, poorly socialized, neglected which will increase their likelihood of aggression. There is no evidence that Pit Bull dogs as a breed, are any more likely to be naturally aggressive than other breeds. However, they may be owned by more lower income families which may not have the resources for early training and neutering. One Notes may be providing more resources in underserved areas. Fighting against breed-specific education is one Notes. Some places (Denver, CO. Miami, FL. Council Bluffs, IA among others) outright ban the ownership of Pit Bulls. Many home-owners policies include a caveat that they can refuse coverage it you own specific breeds of dogs including Pit Bulls.

Dog Fights Article

As the link above indicates, there is little evidence that these laws make communities safer. They are expensive and difficult to regulate. Instead, the CDC has advocated for a breed-neutral approach that include

  • Enhanced enforcement of dog license laws
  • Increased availability to low-cost sterilization (spay/neuter) services
  • Dangerous dog laws that are breed-neutral and focus on the behavior of the individual guardian and dog
  • Graduated penalties and options for dogs deemed dangerous
  • Laws that hold dog guardians financially accountable for failure to adhere to animal control laws
  • Laws that hold dog guardians civilly and criminally liable for unjustified injuries or damage caused by their dogs
  • Laws that prohibit chaining, tethering and unreasonable confinement, coupled with enhanced enforcement of animal cruelty and animal fighting laws
  • Community-based approaches to resolving reckless guardian/dangerous dog questions that encompass all stakeholders, available dog bite data and recommended realistic and enforceable policies
    • Softening legislation for dogs that have passed the American Temperament Test Society test may also be another Notes
